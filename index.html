Cholera ‚Äî what it is, how to prevent it, and how it shaped Thailand's history

Cholera is a water- and food-borne disease caused by Vibrio cholerae. It can cause sudden, severe watery diarrhea and dehydration but is highly treatable with prompt rehydration. Basics

Cholera spreads through ingestion of water or food contaminated by feces containing toxigenic V. cholerae. People in areas with unsafe water, poor sanitation, or inadequate hygiene are at highest risk. Many infected people have no symptoms but can shed bacteria for days. Symptoms typically begin 12 hours to 5 days after exposure. Early oral rehydration saves lives; with organized care, mortality should be below 1%. Sources: WHO, CDC, PAHO

Transmission & risk

  • Contaminated drinking water or ice; raw/undercooked seafood; food washed with unsafe water.
  • Outbreaks follow floods, infrastructure disruption, or crowded settings with limited sanitation.
  • Short-term carriers can shed bacteria for 1‚Äì10 days even without symptoms.

Prevention at a glance

  • Drink safe water (boiled, treated, or bottled); keep it covered.
  • Eat food that is cooked and served hot; avoid raw/undercooked seafood.
  • Wash hands with soap: before eating/preparing food and after using the toilet.
  • Use safe sanitation; treat/dispose of stool safely.
  • Oral cholera vaccines (OCV) are used in endemic areas and outbreak response; travelers to areas with active transmission may be advised vaccination.

Symptoms & warning signs

  • Sudden, profuse watery diarrhea (‚Äúrice-water‚Äù stools).
  • Vomiting, thirst, leg cramps; signs of dehydration (dry mouth, sunken eyes, little or no urination).
  • Severe cases can lead to shock and death within hours if untreated.
If you suspect cholera: start oral rehydration solution (ORS) immediately and seek medical care. Children with diarrhea benefit from zinc as advised by clinicians.

Practical prevention

Water, Sanitation & Hygiene (WASH)

  1. Boil or properly chlorinate water; store in clean, closed containers.
  2. Use latrines; clean high-touch surfaces regularly.
  3. Food safety: cook thoroughly, serve hot, peel it or leave it.

Oral Cholera Vaccines (OCV)

WHO-prequalified OCVs (e.g., Dukoral¬Æ, Euvichol-Plus¬Æ, Euvichol-S¬Æ) are used to protect communities during outbreaks and in endemic settings. U.S.-approved Vaxchora is recommended for certain travelers (ages 2‚Äì64) to areas with active transmission. Ask local health authorities for current guidance.

Treatment

  • Immediate rehydration is lifesaving: ORS for all; IV fluids for severe dehydration.
  • Antibiotics for severe cases as per local protocols; manage electrolytes.
  • With prompt, organized care, case-fatality rates should be < 1%.

Today (2025)

Cholera remains a global challenge. From 1 Jan‚Äì17 Aug 2025, WHO received reports of 409,222 cases and 4,738 deaths from 31 countries, with fluctuating risk by region and season. Continuous investment in WASH, rapid case management, surveillance, and targeted OCV use is essential.

What to do (quick checklist)

  • Only drink water that is boiled, treated, or sealed; use safe ice.
  • Eat cooked food served hot; avoid raw/undercooked seafood.
  • Wash hands with soap frequently; keep toilets and shared surfaces clean.
  • At first sign of watery diarrhea: start ORS; seek medical care promptly.
  • Ask local health authorities about current OCV guidance if you live in or travel to areas with active transmission.
